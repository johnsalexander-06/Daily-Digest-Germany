The first midair helicopter hoist rescue began with a phone call nobody expected

On 29 November 1945, Dmitry "Jimmy" Viner was at the Sikorsky Aircraft Company doing nothing as important as what his next job would entail. The telephone rang and Viner answered it: two men needed rescuing from a barge that had separated from an oil tanker and become trapped on Penfield Reef, off Fairfield, Connecticut, in weather no boat could work. Viner flew out and hoisted both men to safety — the first rescue hoist ever performed from a helicopter in flight, and the origin of a capability that has since saved tens of thousands of lives.

Air & Space Forces Magazine · 15 Jul

Seventy-two years ago this month, an airplane nobody had ordered changed everything

On 15 July 1954 the Boeing Model 367-80 made its first flight with company test pilot A.M. "Tex" Johnston at the controls. Boeing had built it on its own money, without a customer. It became the prototype for the Air Force's C/KC-135 series — the tanker fleet still holding up global reach seven decades later — and the progenitor of the 707, the first civilian jetliner to see wide use. Johnston would later barrel-roll the aircraft over Lake Washington in front of airline executives, and keep his job.
